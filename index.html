Probing the Geometry of Diffusion Models with the String Method

Elio Moreau1, Florentin Coeurdoux1, Grégoire Ferré1, Eric Vanden-Eijnden1, 2
1Capital Fund Management 2Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences, New York University, New York
Paper arXiv

TL;DR We apply the string method to pretrained diffusion models to compute meaningful paths between samples -- without retraining. We compare three path constructions -- generative transport, minimum energy paths (MEPs), and entropy-aware paths -- and argue that the right choice depends on what you care about: morphing, likelihood, or geometry. Our main finding is that entropy matters in high dimension: MEPs can pass through high-likelihood but visually implausible images, while entropy-aware paths stay close to the typical set and produce realistic transitions. For proteins, the framework yields conformational pathways from models trained only on static structures -- and projections onto TICs confirm these paths are physically plausible.

Abstract

Understanding the geometry of learned distributions is fundamental to improving and interpreting diffusion models, yet systematic tools for exploring their landscape remain limited. Standard latent- space interpolations fail to respect the structure of the learned distribution, often traversing low-density regions. We introduce a framework based on the string method that computes continuous paths between samples by evolving curves under the learned score function. Operating on pretrained models without retraining, our approach interpolates between three regimes: pure generative transport, which yields continuous sample paths; gradient-dominated dynamics, which recover minimum energy paths (MEPs); and finite-temperature string dynamics, which compute principal curves—self- consistent paths that balance energy and entropy. We demonstrate that the choice of regime matters in practice. For image diffusion models, MEPs contain high-likelihood but unrealistic “cartoon” images, confirming prior observations that likelihood maxima appear unrealistic; principal curves instead yield realistic morphing sequences despite lower likelihood. For protein structure prediction, our method computes transition pathways between metastable conformers directly from models trained on static structures, yielding paths with physically plausible intermediates. Together, these results establish the string method as a principled tool for probing the modal structure of diffusion models—identifying modes, characterizing barriers, and mapping connectivity in complex learned distributions.

Key Contributions

We adapt the string method from computational chemistry to work with learned score functions, enabling pathway computation from pretrained generative models without explicit energy functions -- and without any retraining or fine-tuning.

We show how three choices of dynamics -- pure transport, gradient-dominated, and finite-temperature -- reveal complementary aspects of the learned landscape, each suited to a different notion of transition.

We demonstrate that accounting for entropy is essential for realistic pathways in high dimensions: principal curves traverse the typical set while MEPs do not. We illustrate this on image morphing and protein conformational transitions, where projections onto TICs confirm the physical plausibility of the entropy-aware paths.

Protein Transition Pathways

BBA and Chignolin are small protein domains widely used as model systems for folding: they fold rapidly, have simple topologies, and are small enough that ground-truth folding pathways are known from simulation [5]. This makes them ideal for validating that computed paths are physically meaningful. We compute minimum energy paths (MEPs) connecting extended and folded conformations using ScoreMD [6], a diffusion model operating in ℝ3 that includes a Fokker–Planck regularization term improving score estimation near t = 1. Projections onto the first two TIC components confirm that the MEPs follow low free-energy routes through configuration space.

Theoretical Framework

The string method classically computes transition pathways in systems with an explicit energy function. Our key contribution is adapting it to the setting of learned score functions, where the energy landscape Vt = −log ρt is time-dependent and only accessible through its gradient st = ∇ log ρt.

Strings as curves of samples. Rather than evolving individual points, we evolve entire curves — strings — of samples under the learned dynamics. A Lagrange multiplier term keeps points equally spaced along the string at all times, preventing them from clustering at attractors rather than tracing a path between endpoints. This is the essential geometric ingredient that makes the method work.

Three regimes, one framework. By controlling how strongly the score enters the dynamics, three path constructions emerge naturally. Pure transport (γt = 0) follows the learned velocity field and produces appealing morphs, but the resulting path depends entirely on initialization and has no intrinsic geometric meaning. Gradient-dominated dynamics (γt ≫ 1, T = 0) recover minimum energy paths (MEPs) — curves along which the score is everywhere tangent, making them maximum likelihood paths between endpoints. Finite-temperature dynamics (γt ≫ 1, T > 0) compute principal curves: self-consistent paths where each point equals the conditional expectation of nearby samples, balancing energy and entropy.

Why entropy matters in high dimensions. MEPs maximize likelihood along the path, but in high dimensions, high likelihood does not imply high probability mass. Probability mass depends on both density and the volume of nearby configuration space — an entropic contribution that dominates as dimension grows. The region where their product is maximized is the typical set, where samples actually concentrate. MEPs pass through modes that lie outside the typical set, producing the “cartoon” images observed in prior work. This is not a model defect but a consequence of concentration of measure. Principal curves resolve this by accounting for entropy, keeping paths within the typical set.

The string method turns a generative model into a geometric instrument: it reveals modes, barriers, and connectivity, and exposes the fundamental tension between likelihood and realism that shapes the landscape in high dimensions.
